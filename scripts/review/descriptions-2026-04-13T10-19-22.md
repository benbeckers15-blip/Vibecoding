## Ad Hoc by Cherubino

**Source:** website  ·  **Words:** 291  ·  **ID:** `ad-hoc-by-cherubino`

**Pull Quote:**
> Larry Cherubino's range-entry wines outperform their price bracket by a wider margin than most in the region.

**¶1 — The Place:**
Ad Hoc operates out of a cellar door open only four days a week, and that limited access is part of the point. This is the entry-level range from Larry Cherubino — a winemaker whose fingerprints are across some of Western Australia's most respected labels — and the tasting experience reflects that: unhurried, focused, with none of the volume-tourism energy that attaches itself to bigger operations along the Bussell Highway.

**¶2 — The Wines:**
The Ad Hoc wines draw on fruit from across Western Australia's south, including Margaret River and the Great Southern, giving Larry Cherubino room to work across varieties that suit each region's strengths. Expect Cabernet Sauvignon and Chardonnay alongside less predictable options — Tempranillo, Gewurztraminer, Pinot Gris — made in a style that stays approachable without being simple. These are not stripped-back commercial wines. The winemaking sensibility that runs through the Cherubino stable — minimal intervention, site-honest fruit — carries through here even at the accessible price point. For a range positioned as an everyday line, the wines hold together with more coherence than most.

**¶3 — The Story:**
Larry Cherubino spent years as head winemaker at Houghton before establishing his own label in 2004, starting with land in the Frankland River sub-region alongside his wife Edwina, a fifth-generation West Australian with deep family ties to the area. Ad Hoc emerged as the more accessible arm of that broader project — a way to work with fruit from multiple regions without the constraints of single-vineyard positioning. The cellar door sits within that wider Cherubino story, and visiting it gives a clearer picture of how deliberately the whole enterprise is structured. For anyone working through the region's producers, this is one of the few stops where a seasoned winemaker's judgment is on show at an honest price.

## After Hours Wine

**Source:** website  ·  **Words:** 291  ·  **ID:** `after-hours-wine`

**Pull Quote:**
> The Chardonnay alone would justify the stop — the competition record speaks plainly.

**¶1 — The Place:**
After Hours runs on a simple idea: small enough to mean it. Around 30 tonnes of fruit each year, roughly 2,500 cases. Owner Warwick answers his own phone. Bookings aren't required unless your group hits six. That scale is not incidental — it shapes everything from the way the wines are handled to the way visitors are received at the new cellar door tasting room, open seven days.

**¶2 — The Wines:**
The vineyard holds Cabernet Sauvignon across 2.6 hectares, Shiraz across 1.6, and roughly equal plantings of Merlot, Semillon, Sauvignon Blanc, and Chardonnay at 1.1 hectares each. Two ranges — After Hours and the more accessible 9 to 5 — work the same fruit at different price points. The medals are not incidental either: the 2016 Chardonnay drew 95 points from the James Halliday Wine Companion and gold at the Australian Cool Climate Wine Show, the National Cool Climate Wine Show, Cairns, Rutherglen, and Winewise. The 2015 Shiraz scored 95 points from Halliday. The 2017 Shiraz took Top in Class at Winestate's Best of the West. Across both reds and whites, the scores cluster consistently in the 93–95 range — not a fluke vintage, but a pattern across more than a decade of competition results.

**¶3 — The Story:**
After Hours sits just 20 minutes from Busselton, Dunsborough, and Vasse, which makes it easy to include without making it feel like a detour. That proximity to the tourist corridor could easily produce a winery built around throughput. It hasn't. The 5.0 rating across 157 Google reviews suggests something more personal is happening here — the kind of visit where the person pouring the wine is also the person who made it. For a region where cellar door experiences can feel increasingly corporate, that directness is worth seeking out.
